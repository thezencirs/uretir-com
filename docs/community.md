# Community Model

## Goal

The Uretir community connects people around credible production knowledge. It should make useful work easier to discover, recognize thoughtful contribution, and protect members from low-quality engagement incentives.

Community is a third-order growth priority after traffic and authority. Community work should therefore strengthen the knowledge system first: better expertise, credible profiles, useful discussion, collection sharing, and durable return reasons. Do not launch social mechanics that create activity without improving either knowledge quality or member value.

Community architecture must remain performance-safe: public knowledge pages cannot depend on expensive social queries to render; engagement data should be progressively loaded, cached, or precomputed where it supports discovery without degrading indexability or reading speed.

## Core objects

| Object | Purpose | Member control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Profile | Professional identity and public context | visibility, edit, export, deletion |
| Follow | Subscribe to a person, company, topic, tool, industry, or factory | follow/unfollow and notification level |
| Bookmark | Save a single item for later | private by default |
| Collection | Organize and optionally share saved items | private, unlisted, or public visibility |
| Comment | Add discussion to a content item | edit window, report, delete own content |
| Verification | Signal that a person or organization met a stated review process | clear criteria and revocation policy |
| Badge | Recognize an observable contribution or status | criteria visible; no paid implication |
| Contribution score | Summarize meaningful participation | transparent inputs and no hidden penalties |

## Profiles

Profiles should communicate enough context to make a connection useful: display name, role, organization, industry, location at an appropriate granularity, bio, links, interests, and verified status where applicable. Do not require a long profile before a member can receive value.

## Following and personalization

Following is an explicit preference signal. The home feed should prioritize follow relationships, saved interests, timely editorial relevance, and diversity of sources. It must provide understandable controls rather than an unexplained black box.

## Collections and bookmarks

Bookmarks are personal memory; collections are personal knowledge organization. A shared collection should show its curator, contents, update date, and visibility status. Sharing must be intentional and reversible.

## Comments and moderation

Comments should make a page more useful, not increase activity for its own sake. Require clear conduct rules, report paths, moderator tools, rate limits, and a transparent escalation policy. Moderation decisions must be logged with enough context for review, while protecting reporter privacy.

## Verification

Verification confirms a defined claim, not general superiority. Examples: verified company, engineer, academic, manufacturer, or expert. Each verification type requires published evidence criteria, a review owner, expiry or re-check rules where necessary, and a revocation process.

## Badges and contribution score

Badges recognize specific achievements such as early membership, verified status, documented expertise, or sustained helpful contribution. Contribution score should use auditable positive signals such as accepted improvements, useful citations, editorial contributions, and community appreciation from trusted contexts. Avoid opaque score decay, engagement farming, and metrics that reward volume over value.

## Safety and privacy

Use privacy-preserving defaults. Profiles should never expose contact details or precise location by default. Members must be able to block, mute, report, and control notifications. Community data needs retention rules, anti-spam safeguards, and an incident process before broad public interaction launches.
